Key
The toning equation is simple: eat enough protein to keep the muscle, eat slightly less calories overall to lose the fat. The biggest mistake women make is eating too little protein (the muscle shrinks along with the fat) or cutting calories too aggressively (metabolism adapts, energy crashes, training suffers). The goal is a 250–350 kcal deficit — enough to lose roughly 0.25–0.5kg of fat per week, without compromising strength or energy in sessions.

Note
These numbers assume ~65kg bodyweight. If significantly lighter or heavier, scale protein proportionally (~1.8–2.0g per kg). Do not drop below 1,500 kcal — below this threshold training quality suffers, muscle is lost alongside fat, and the body adapts by reducing metabolic rate. Slow and steady beats aggressive restriction every time for long-term results.
Daily meal structure
~7:30–8:00
Breakfast
~35–40g protein
  • 4 whole eggs scrambled + 200g Greek yoghurt on the side — ~38g protein
  • Or: 3-egg omelette with cheese + 150g cottage cheese — ~36g protein
  • Or: 200g Greek yoghurt (0%) + 30g protein powder blended — ~40g protein
  • Or: 3-egg omelette + 150g cottage cheese on the side — ~36g protein
  • Add: oats or sourdough toast (50g) for carbs, plus fruit
  • Coffee or tea fine — no limit on non-caloric drinks
On training days (Days 1, 2, 3) include a full carb portion here — you need the energy. On rest days, a lighter carb portion is fine.
~12:30–13:00
Lunch
~35–40g protein
  • 150g firm tofu (pan-fried) + 150g cottage cheese, or 4 eggs + 100g halloumi
  • Or: large lentil or chickpea salad (~200g cooked) + 2 boiled eggs + feta
  • Large salad or roasted vegetables — no limit on these
  • Small portion of carbs: 60–80g rice, sweet potato, or lentils (cooked weight)
  • Olive oil dressing — keep fat moderate, not zero
This is the easiest meal to prep in advance. Batch cook protein and grains on Sunday. Having it ready removes the decision-making that leads to poor choices.
~18:30–19:30
Dinner
~35–45g protein
  • 200g firm tofu or tempeh (stir-fry, bake, or pan-fry — tempeh has more protein and a meatier texture)
  • Or: 5-egg frittata with spinach + 100g feta or parmesan
  • Or: lentil/black bean dish (~250g cooked) + 150g Greek yoghurt on the side
  • Large portion of vegetables: broccoli, spinach, asparagus, courgette, peppers
  • Moderate carbs: 60–80g rice or sweet potato on training days. Lighter on rest days.
  • Optional dessert: 150g Greek yoghurt or 150g cottage cheese — adds ~15–18g more protein
Eating a proper dinner is important — don't skip it to save calories. People who skip dinner tend to overeat late at night. A structured dinner closes the day and removes the temptation.
Timing around training
Before session
Eat normally. Don't train fasted for toning. A proper meal 1–2 hrs before means better performance = better results.
Within 60 min after
Protein + carbs. Next meal or a shake with banana. Closes the anabolic window and starts recovery.
On rest days
Keep protein the same. Reduce carbs slightly (−30–50g). Fat and protein stay constant.
Pre-sleep
150g cottage cheese or Greek yoghurt — casein protein feeds muscle repair overnight. Low calorie, high impact.
Supplements — keep it simple
Tier 1 — Worth Doing
Strong evidence for toning goals
Supplement Dose & Timing Why it matters for toning Tier
Creatine Monohydrate
3–5g daily
with food
 Many women avoid creatine thinking it causes bloating or bulk — it does neither. It improves strength output in sessions (meaning better stimulus = better tone) and improves body composition. One of the few supplements with unambiguous evidence. Take it daily. Tier 1
Vitamin D3
2,000–3,000 IU
with food, morning
 Deficiency is extremely common and suppresses energy, mood, immune function, and muscle performance. Especially important if you're indoors most of the day. Take with a meal containing fat for proper absorption. Tier 1
Algae Omega-3
1–2g EPA+DHA
with food
 The vegetarian source of omega-3s — algae is actually where fish get their EPA and DHA from, so this is the direct source. Just as effective as fish oil, and no fish required. Look for "algae-derived EPA+DHA" on the label. Available in most health food shops and online. Tier 1
Magnesium Glycinate
200–300mg
before bed
 Supports sleep quality (critical for recovery), reduces muscle tension and cramping, and helps with stress management. Glycinate form is gentlest on digestion. Most people are low in magnesium without realising it. Tier 1
Vitamin B12
250–500µg daily
or 2,000µg weekly
 B12 is found almost exclusively in animal products — vegetarians who eat eggs and dairy get some, but not reliably enough. Deficiency develops slowly over years and causes fatigue, nerve issues, and anaemia. Non-negotiable for vegetarians. Methylcobalamin form is best absorbed. A weekly high-dose tablet is just as effective as daily low-dose. Tier 1
Tier 2 — Useful if Needed
Use as tools, not foundations
Supplement Dose & Timing When to use it Tier
Whey Protein Powder
25–30g per serving
post-training or with meals
 Only needed if consistently struggling to hit protein targets from food. Not magic — it's just convenient protein. Whey is fast-absorbing and leucine-rich. Casein (in cottage cheese) is better for pre-sleep use. Don't rely on shakes when whole food is available. Tier 2
Iron (if deficient)
As prescribed
with vitamin C
 Iron deficiency is common in women and causes chronic fatigue that is often mistaken for poor fitness. If energy is persistently low despite good sleep and nutrition, get blood work. Only supplement if a test confirms deficiency — excess iron is harmful. Take with vitamin C for absorption. Tier 2

Common mistakes to avoid
Avoid
Cutting carbs entirely. Carbohydrates fuel training. Low-carb approaches work for weight loss but reduce training performance, which reduces muscle stimulus, which undermines the whole toning goal. Keep carbs — time them around workouts.
Avoid
Eating too little. A 500–700 kcal deficit feels like fast progress but causes muscle loss, low energy, and metabolic adaptation within weeks. A 250–350 kcal deficit is slower but sustainable and preserves the muscle that creates the toned look.
Avoid
Measuring progress only on the scale. When building muscle and losing fat simultaneously, the scale often stays flat or moves slowly even when the body is visibly changing. Use measurements, photos every 2–3 weeks, and how clothes fit as primary indicators.
Avoid
Skipping protein at breakfast. Eating 35–40g protein at breakfast increases satiety, reduces cravings throughout the day, and ensures the protein total is achievable across three normal meals. A high-carb, low-protein breakfast (cereal, toast, fruit only) makes hitting the daily protein target very difficult.
Tracking tip: For the first two weeks, log food in any app (Cronometer is free and accurate) just to calibrate portion sizes. You don't need to track forever — most people find that after 2–3 weeks they have a solid intuition for whether a meal hits the protein target. The goal is to understand what 35g of protein looks like on a plate, not to count calories indefinitely.
